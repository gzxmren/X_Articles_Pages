How the market works

Each weather market on Polymarket is a set of discrete temperature buckets. Each bucket trades as a contract between $0.01 and $1.00. If that bucket is the correct outcome, it pays exactly $1.00. All others pay zero. The prices across all buckets roughly sum to $1 - they represent the crowd's implicit probability distribution.
The chart above shows a typical mispricing pattern. The crowd anchors on the phone app's point estimate and overweights the central buckets. The ensemble model - which accounts for uncertainty, warm-air advection, and model spread - shows fat tails. The tail buckets are systematically underpriced.
If the model says a bucket has an 18% true probability but the market prices it at 7¢, the expected value per dollar risked is +$1.57. You don't need to win every trade. You need to be consistently less wrong than the crowd on where the tails sit.

The math behind it

The core technique is deceptively simple. Take a weather forecast. Assume it carries some uncertainty - typically +-3–4°F for a 24-hour window. Simulate tens of thousands of possible outcomes around that forecast. Count what fraction land in each market bucket. That's your model probability distribution.
The key insight is sigma - the uncertainty parameter. A naive trader uses the point forecast ("61°F tomorrow"). A quant asks: how spread out is the model ensemble? If ECMWF's 51 runs disagree by 5°F, your sigma should be 5°F. Wider sigma = fatter tails = bigger mispricings in the extreme buckets.
In simple terms: quant looks at the range of forecasts, if the models show temperatures ranging from 56°F to 66°F, that means there is a high degree of uncertainty.

Why tails are always mispriced

Human intuition is poor at probability tails. When a weather app says "High of 62°F," nobody buys the 72°F bucket - even if the ensemble shows a 12% chance of a warm air surge. The market price on that bucket might be 3¢. The model says 12¢. That's a 4x mispricing, and it appears in nearly every market, in nearly every city, every single day.

The laddering strategy

The traders who make the most consistent money don't bet on a single bucket. They ladder - buying multiple buckets across a range, sizing each position according to the edge, and accepting that most legs will expire worthless. The math still works because the winners pay multiples.
