WHY THIS ADVISORY

This note steps back from our normal SPX options structure analysis to address something broader. Over the past week, several asset classes have moved in ways that, taken together, present an unusual risk configuration. We want you to see what we see, clearly and without exaggeration, so you can make your own informed decisions heading into next week.
We describe the forces. You decide what to do with them.

THE FACTS

1. SPX Options Structure: Four-Greek Bearish Convergence on Monday

The Mar 9 weekly expiration carries the first full four-Greek bearish convergence of this cycle. All four market maker hedging channels — gamma, delta, vanna, and charm — point in the same direction: sell. This is the structural configuration our framework identifies as maximum danger for a single expiration. The detailed analysis was published in the v5 advisory on Thursday.
The Mar 9 convergence is followed by a secondary danger node on Mar 13 (GEX -11.5, DEX +1,323). The first genuinely constructive session is Mar 11 (DEX -1,522, dealers buy). The SPX options structure provides timing and sequence for the week ahead, and it is unambiguously bearish through Monday, with relief on Tuesday-Wednesday and renewed pressure Thursday.

2. VIX Dealer Positioning Caps Volatility Through Mar 18

The Mar 18 VIX monthly expiration carries 2.3 million contracts with significant negative dealer delta (-18,345 DEX). This means VIX dealers are positioned to sell VIX rallies — creating a structural cap on volatility spikes through Mar 18 settlement. This is the mechanism behind what practitioner Cem Karsan describes as an "orderly decline, pinned by dealer positioning."
After Mar 18, these 2.3 million contracts and all associated dealer hedges are removed. VIX becomes structurally unanchored. The Mar 20 monthly SPX options expiration — which carries the largest dealer selling exposure on the board (DEX +68,210) — occurs two days later, in a VIX environment with no dealer damping.
There is a paradox here worth noting. Historically, major market bottoms tend to coincide with VIX spikes well above 40 — the capitulation washout that exhausts selling. At VIX 29, we are not there. The VIX dealer positioning is structurally preventing VIX from reaching the 40+ levels that would signal a genuine washout. In effect, the dealer anchor may be suppressing the very capitulation event that would create a durable bottom. After Mar 18, when the anchor is removed, VIX becomes free to reach those levels — and that may be where the actual capitulation occurs, not before.

3. The Bond Safe Haven Is Not Functioning

In a normal geopolitical crisis, Treasury bonds rally as investors seek safety. This week, the opposite occurred. Treasury bond prices declined 2.03% and 10-year yields rose 17 basis points to 4.13%, even as equities sold off and crude oil spiked.
The mechanism is straightforward: the Strait of Hormuz closure has driven crude to $91.27, the highest level since September 2023. Rising oil feeds directly into inflation expectations, which pushes rate cut expectations further out, which makes long-duration bonds less attractive. The safe-haven function breaks when the crisis itself is inflationary — Treasuries cannot absorb flight-to-quality flows when the inflation risk generated by the crisis makes those bonds less valuable.
Nations Indexes confirms this from the options side: Treasury Bond PutDex surged 46% this week (traders buying downside protection on bonds) while Treasury Bond CallDex fell 5.59% (traders not buying upside exposure). Bond option traders expect yields to continue rising.

4. The US Dollar Has Undergone a Structural Regime Transition — Not Just a Crisis Spike

The dollar rallied 0.95% this week — the only traditional safe-haven asset that performed its historical function. Gold declined 1.95% and silver fell 10.40%, not because of forced liquidation but because the sharp dollar rally mechanically reprices all non-USD assets lower.
What makes this particularly significant is that the dollar's move is not merely a temporary flight-to-quality reaction. Hedgeye Risk Management's fractal Risk Range model — which uses price, volume, and volatility across multiple timeframes — confirmed the US Dollar Index (
DXY
) as Bullish on both TRADE (short-term) and TREND (intermediate-term) durations as of March 4. This is a structural phase transition. The dollar had been Bearish on both durations for weeks, flipped to Neutral, and then confirmed a full Bullish breakout in rapid succession. The crisis accelerated a trend that was already forming.
This distinction matters. A crisis-driven dollar spike fades when the crisis fades. A structural dollar bull trend persists independently. Every non-USD risk asset — equities, commodities, emerging markets, credit — now faces a headwind that does not require the geopolitical crisis to continue in order to remain operative. Even a ceasefire or Hormuz reopening would not automatically reverse the dollar's confirmed bullish trend, though it would remove the acceleration component.
The transmission is direct: when the dollar strengthens, global liquidity tightens, borrowing costs rise for dollar-denominated debt worldwide, risk appetite diminishes, and capital flows out of riskier assets. The dollar is the currency in which the world settles debts, prices commodities, and moves capital. When it moves, everything else adjusts.

5. Options Sentiment Is at Historic Extremes

Nations Indexes S&P 500 RiskDex — which measures the ratio of out-of-the-money put costs to out-of-the-money call costs — closed at 15.70 this week. In more than 20 years of daily data, it has closed above this level only twice: August 11, 2017 and March 13, 2020. Simultaneously, S&P 500 CallDex (measuring demand for upside call options) fell to its lowest level since September 2021.
The Nations Optimism Index fell 59.68% to 19.25, near its floor. The VolDex term structure is extremely inverted — shorter-dated volatility measures are well above longer-dated ones, indicating the market is pricing acute near-term risk rather than a gradual deterioration.
Nasdaq-100 RiskDex reached 11.06 — the highest level ever recorded outside of March 2020.

6. The Stabilizing "Gamma Grip" Is Degrading

Independent analysis from options structure researcher Ben Kizemchuk identifies a critical shift in market internals: implied dispersion (DSPX) is falling while implied correlation (
C
OR1M) is rising. This combination means individual stocks are moving more in unison rather than independently. When this occurs, the dealer hedging mechanism that has suppressed S&P 500 volatility since October 2025 — the "gamma grip" — loses effectiveness.
Under normal conditions, dealers hedge index gamma partly through single-stock dispersion. When dispersion collapses, all hedging pressure concentrates into SPX index delta. The stabilizing "buy dips, sell rips" behavior weakens. On selloffs, dealer hedging can shift from counter-cyclical (stabilizing) to pro-cyclical (amplifying).

7. The Macro Backdrop Is Stagflationary — Removing the Fed Put

Friday's nonfarm payrolls report showed the US economy shed 92,000 jobs in February — a significant miss against expectations of +60,000. This arrived in the same week that crude oil surged 35.64% to $91.27. The combination of collapsing employment and spiking energy costs is stagflationary: inflation is rising while the economy is weakening.
This matters because it removes the Federal Reserve as a backstop. In a normal equity selloff, the market can price in rate cuts as a safety net — the "Fed put." In a stagflationary environment, the Fed faces a bind: it cannot cut rates (oil-driven inflation would worsen) and it cannot tighten further (the labor market is deteriorating). The central bank is effectively sidelined, which removes one more cushion that normally limits equity downside.

8. Early Signs of Liquidity Stress in Private Credit

On March 6, BlackRock limited withdrawals from its $26 billion HPS Corporate Lending Fund after redemption requests reached approximately $1.2 billion (9.3% of assets), capping payouts at 5% (~$620 million). This is a gate — a mechanism that restricts investor access to their capital during periods of stress.
This is relevant beyond the private credit space because gated funds can generate contagion. When investors cannot redeem from illiquid vehicles, they sell liquid assets — public equities, Treasuries, investment-grade bonds — to raise cash. If redemption pressure spreads to other non-traded funds in the $1.8 trillion private credit space, the forced selling of liquid assets adds another layer of supply to already stressed public markets. It may also contribute to the unusual pattern of bonds and gold selling alongside equities — not all of that selling is explained by the dollar rally alone.

WHAT MAKES THIS CONFIGURATION UNUSUAL

Each of these eight factors has occurred independently at various points in market history. What makes the current setup noteworthy is their simultaneous convergence:
  • SPX options structure is bearish (four-Greek convergence)
  • VIX is anchored but the anchor has a known expiration date (Mar 18) — and may be suppressing the capitulation that would create a bottom
  • Bonds are selling alongside equities (inflation transmission)
  • The dollar has undergone a confirmed structural regime transition to Bullish, tightening global liquidity independently of the crisis
  • Options sentiment is at 20-year extremes (RiskDex)
  • The gamma stabilization mechanism is degrading (dispersion-correlation shift)
  • The macro backdrop is stagflationary, sidelining the Fed as a backstop
  • Early liquidity stress in private credit introduces a potential forced-selling channel into public markets
These are eight independent risk factors using different methodologies and data sources. They do not normally align simultaneously.

WHAT COULD CHANGE THIS PICTURE

The current configuration is driven primarily by the geopolitical catalyst and its oil market consequences. The most direct paths to resolution:
  • Ceasefire or Strait of Hormuz reopening would remove the oil shock, restore the bond safe haven, ease dollar pressure, and defuse the inflation transmission chain. Multiple risk factors would resolve simultaneously. Specific mechanisms to monitor: the effectiveness of US naval escort operations and maritime insurance reinstatement for Hormuz transit, OPEC+ emergency supply response, and any diplomatic channel between the US and Iran regarding shipping lane access. A credible reopening of tanker traffic — even partial — would deflate crude rapidly and cascade through the entire cross-asset configuration.
  • Crude oil sustaining below $85 would weaken the inflation transmission even without full geopolitical resolution, potentially restoring some bond safe-haven function. A Strategic Petroleum Reserve release or coordinated IEA emergency stock drawdown could achieve this independently of Hormuz status.
  • VIX spike above 35-40 that exhausts put demand could create a washout event where RiskDex mean-reverts from extreme levels. Historically, RiskDex extremes do resolve — the question is whether they resolve through stabilization or through a flush that creates the bottom.
  • SPX holds above 6750 through Monday would suggest the Mar 9 convergence is being absorbed by the market despite the hostile macro environment.
A note on speed: the same interconnection that makes this configuration dangerous also means it can unwind rapidly. Because the bearish factors are linked through a single causal chain (geopolitical crisis to oil to inflation to bonds to dollar to equities), a credible resolution at the source would cascade through multiple channels simultaneously. The Mar 11 relief node in the SPX options structure provides a natural window where a positive geopolitical development would find receptive dealer positioning (DEX -1,522, dealers buy).
One caveat: the dollar's confirmed structural trend transition to Bullish means that even if the geopolitical catalyst resolves, the USD headwind to risk assets does not automatically reverse. A ceasefire would remove the crisis acceleration but the underlying dollar bull trend would need to be independently broken by the data — weaker US growth, a dovish Fed pivot, or a reversal in capital flows — before the global liquidity tightening eases fully.

OUR POSTURE

We are not calling a crash. We are not recommending specific trades. We are describing a set of structural forces that are unusually aligned and that, in our assessment, warrant heightened awareness heading into the Mar 9-20 window.
The SPX options structure provides the timing and sequence. The macro environment determines whether those structural events produce contained moves or amplified ones. Right now, the macro environment is not providing the cushions it normally does.
