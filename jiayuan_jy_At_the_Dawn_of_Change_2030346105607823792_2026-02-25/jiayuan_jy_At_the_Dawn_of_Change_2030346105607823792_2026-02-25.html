This isn't incremental efficiency gains. The core explosion is this:
AI Coding (or more broadly, agents) has crossed the baseline and is being rapidly commoditized. Programming is no longer a scarce skill requiring years of training. It's becoming an on-demand resource at near-zero marginal cost.
But amid this acceleration, one thing made me stop and think: this isn't the first time.
Throughout history, whenever a high-barrier capability suddenly becomes cheap and widely available, it triggers a predictable series of structural changes—old professions decline, new ones emerge, value chains reorganize, power nodes shift. History won't tell us "what to do," but it can tell us "what's wrong."
This article goes back to those historical moments to see what actually happened. Then we return to the present to see what it helps us understand.

When Copying Became Free

Before Gutenberg, every book in Europe had to be copied by hand, letter by letter, by monastery scribes. A handwritten Bible cost about three years of a clerk's salary. The total number of books in all of Europe was roughly 30,000. The "copying" of knowledge was an expensive capability monopolized by the Church and a few elites.
Around 1440, Gutenberg independently developed a practical metal movable type printing system in Europe (the principle of movable type was first invented by Bi Sheng in Song Dynasty China around 1040). By 1455, the first Gutenberg Bible was printed. Book prices then dropped at about 2.4% annually for over a century, falling by two-thirds by 1500.
A key competitive dynamic: when a new printer entered a city market, local book prices immediately dropped about 25%. By 1480, 110 European cities had printing presses. By 1500, that number exceeded 236, and total books exploded from 30,000 to 10-20 million.
The supply side exploded. But the consequences went far beyond "more books":
  • Old professions declined: Demand for scribes collapsed. Monastery scriptoria withered within decades. In 1492, Abbot Johannes Trithemius wrote In Praise of Scribes, trying to argue for the spiritual value of handwriting.
  • New professions emerged: Printing created an entire new industry chain—typesetters, proofreaders, bookbinders, illustrators, publishers, booksellers. None of these existed before Gutenberg.
  • Supply glut and quality variance: Low-quality printed materials flooded the market—religious pamphlets, prophecy books, erotica.
  • Unforeseen second-order effects: The Reformation (Luther used printing to spread ideas at scale), the Scientific Revolution (academic papers could circulate across borders), the rise of nation-states (vernacular publications strengthened national identity)—none of this was something Gutenberg could have predicted.
This story reveals a recurring pattern. Clayton Christensen proposed the "Law of Conservation of Attractive Profits": when one layer of the value chain gets commoditized and profits disappear, adjacent layers produce new proprietary products to capture profit.
Ben Thompson expressed this logic more bluntly when analyzing Netflix: "Breaking apart an integrated system—commoditizing and modularizing it—destroys incumbent value while enabling new entrants to integrate at a different layer and capture new value."

Value doesn't disappear. It migrates. After "copying" was commoditized, value moved from "transcription" to "content creation" and "curation/distribution." Publishers—not printers—became the new power nodes.
Commoditization has another often-overlooked structural consequence: when supply explodes, demand (attention, budget, time) doesn't grow proportionally. The result is extreme power-law distribution—a tiny number of winners capture most of the value, while the long tail of output goes largely unseen.
Fifty years after printing, Europe went from 30,000 to 20 million books. But only a tiny fraction became enduring classics. In a world of supply glut, attention itself becomes the scarcest resource.
This supply-side explosion is replaying now. a16z data shows iOS app releases in December 2025 grew 60% year-over-year, with 24% cumulative growth over the past 12 months—attributed to the rise of agentic coding (also called "vibe coding"). Just like the app explosion after the iPhone SDK launched in 2008: when creation barriers drop, supply always explodes.

When Power Became Cheap

In the late 19th century, factory power came from steam engines or waterwheels. Entire factory layouts were designed around a massive central line shaft: the steam engine in the basement turned the main shaft, which drove machines on every floor through belts. Factories had to be built as narrow, multi-story buildings with all machines clustered near the shaft. Building a factory required enormous capital—not just for machines, but for the power system itself.
A panoramic view of the factory floor. The ceiling is dominated by drive shafts and belts, blocking natural light and creating a dim working environment. The machinery layout is designed entirely around power transmission rather than the production flow.
Electricity changed everything. The grid let any factory "plug and play" for power without building its own steam engine. In 1899, electric motors accounted for just 5% of US manufacturing power. By 1909, 23%. By 1929, 77%.
The transition happened in three stages:
  • first, large electric motors replaced steam engines to drive existing shafts;
  • then machines were grouped with smaller motors for each group;
  • finally, the shaft was eliminated entirely—each machine got its own motor
But here's an extremely important lesson.
Economist Paul David's famous 1990 paper "The Dynamo and the Computer: An Historical Perspective on the Modern Productivity Paradox" showed that from electricity's commercialization (1881, when power stations opened in New York and London) to measurable economic productivity gains (the 1920s), there was a 40-year lag. An observer in 1900 would find almost no evidence that the "electric revolution" was making business more efficient.
Why? Because early factories just swapped steam engines for electric motors. Everything else stayed the same—layout, processes, organization. They were using new tools to do old things.
The real productivity explosion came in the 1920s—manufacturing total factor productivity (TFP) growth hit about 5% annually, accounting for 84% of the entire economy's TFP growth—when a new generation of factories was designed entirely around electricity's characteristics: single-story buildings replaced multi-story ones, machines could be arranged by workflow rather than power transmission, factories became brighter and safer. This ultimately enabled Ford's assembly line. Ford's factory wasn't "an old factory powered by electricity." It was "an entirely new production system designed around electricity's characteristics."
This echoes Robert Solow's 1987 IT productivity paradox across a century—"You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." Erik Brynjolfsson's 1993 research confirmed: despite US computing power increasing a hundredfold in the 1970s-80s, labor productivity growth fell from over 3% annually in the 1960s to about 1%. Productivity only rises when technology investment is accompanied by complementary organizational change—just like the electricity story.
The same paradox is replaying in AI coding. A rigorous randomized controlled trial by METR in 2025 found that when 16 experienced open-source developers used AI tools on projects they knew well (averaging 5 years of maintenance), task completion time was actually 19% slower—while they had predicted 24% faster before the experiment. Larger surveys show 75% of engineers use AI tools, but most organizations see no measurable performance gains. Why? AI accelerates code generation but creates new bottlenecks in code review, integration, and testing—like speeding up just one machine on an assembly line. You don't get a faster factory. You get a bigger pile-up.

But this doesn't mean AI coding has no value. The key is who's using it and how.
Karpathy's example—a weekend project compressed to 30 minutes—shows exactly this: when the user has sufficient system architecture ability and judgment, AI is an enormous lever. The METR developers slowing down on "familiar projects" may have been precisely because their old workflows weren't optimized for AI. Real efficiency gains require redesigning your entire way of working around AI's characteristics—just like electricity.

When Barriers Collapse

Before AWS, building an internet service meant buying servers, renting data center space, hiring ops teams. Marc Andreessen recalled in "Why Software Is Eating the World": in 2000, his partner Ben Horowitz was CEO of Loudcloud, where running a basic internet application cost a customer about $150,000 per month.
In 2006, AWS launched S3 and EC2. By 2011, the same application cost about $1,500 per month on AWS—a 100x cost reduction. AWS made over 60 price cuts between 2006 and 2014. S3 storage costs fell 86% over 12 years (from $0.15/GB to $0.022/GB).
The barrier collapse triggered a startup explosion. The capital needed to launch an internet company dropped from millions to a few thousand dollars. Y Combinator could start in 2005 with tiny seed checks (~$20,000 initially) precisely because infrastructure costs had transformed. Instagram had 13 employees when Facebook acquired it for $1 billion. Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe—these companies exist because they didn't need to build data centers.
The SaaS market grew from $31.4 billion in 2015 to over $250 billion in 2024, with over 16,500 SaaS companies in the US alone. But each vertical ultimately converges to 2-3 winners—another power-law distribution, following the same pattern as post-printing supply explosions. Value migrated from "having servers" to "having users" to "having data flywheels" and "having network effects."

This supply-side explosion comes with another recurring cycle: first Unbundle, then Re-bundle.
Jim Barksdale famously said: "There are only two ways to make money in business: One is to bundle; the other is to unbundle." When a capability becomes cheap, integrated solutions get broken into smaller, focused products. But when fragmentation reaches an extreme, new integrators emerge to recombine these fragments into a new integrated experience.
This cycle has played out repeatedly:
  • Printing unbundled the Church's knowledge monopoly; then publishers re-bundled content curation and distribution.
  • Cloud computing unbundled IT infrastructure; then AWS/GCP/Azure re-bundled into new integrated cloud platforms.
  • News was unbundled by blogs and social media—journalists could bypass newspapers, readers could consume single articles instead of full subscriptions. Then Substack and paid newsletters re-bundled independent writing: writers got direct subscriber relationships, readers got curated content packages. Value migrated from "owning presses" to "owning reader trust."

The Laws of Commoditization

Three stories spanning centuries—printing press, electric motor, cloud server—follow the same pattern:
AI is commoditizing coding. But it's not commoditizing "what problem to solve." When "how to implement" is no longer the bottleneck, "what to implement" and "for whom" become potential differentiators.
The same power-law distribution is emerging in AI agents: countless copycats appear, but winner-take-all effects are extreme—not because followers are bad, but because in a supply-glut world, attention itself is the scarcest resource. Using AI to build yesterday's SaaS faster—"AI helps you build a CRM quicker"—is essentially swapping a steam engine for an electric motor. Very low defensibility. The real opportunity is redesigning product forms around the new reality of zero-marginal-cost code production.
AI coding is currently in an unbundling phase: standardized tools are losing value, while long-tail, personalized custom tools gain value. But history tells us re-bundling will follow.

Where We Are Now

Economist Carlota Perez proposed an influential framework describing how every technological revolution passes through two major phases.
  • Installation Period: New technology enters the market, infrastructure gets built, financial capital floods in, speculation bubbles form. This phase is characterized by chaos, experimentation, overinvestment.
  • Turning Point: The bubble bursts, recession hits, institutional frameworks begin adjusting to the new technology.
  • Deployment Period: Technology gets widely adopted into mainstream society. If institutions adapt well, a "golden age" follows—the technology's full potential is unleashed.
(Source:
https://avc.com/2015/02/the-carlota-perez-framework/
)
If Perez's framework holds, AI is currently in the early Installation period—massive capital inflows, labs everywhere, crowded consensus tracks. What we're seeing matches this phase perfectly: supply glut, countless copycats, extreme winner-take-all effects. The latter half of Installation usually brings a speculative bubble. After the bubble bursts, the real "Deployment period" begins—when infrastructure has matured, institutions have adapted, and the technology's full potential starts to be released. According to this framework, the biggest value creation typically happens in Deployment, not Installation.

What Might Happen

Programmers won't disappear, but the definition of "programmer" will change. Scribes didn't vanish overnight—manuscripts were still being commissioned decades after printing. Steam-powered factories didn't immediately disappear after electrification. But competitive differentiation will shift from "can you write code" to "system design and architectural judgment."
There's an important distinction here: technology replaces "tasks," not "people." Work that can be broken into clear steps—whether cognitive (data entry) or physical (assembly line)—gets replaced by technology. Work requiring judgment, creativity, complex communication gets amplified by technology. The result: high-end skills become more valuable, mid-tier skills get commoditized, and practitioners are squeezed toward the extremes.
Similarly, programmers' value will migrate from "writing code"—an increasingly routine task—to system architecture judgment, product intuition, taste, and debugging/integrating complex systems—which remain non-routine tasks.
The biggest winners won't be "people who code fastest with AI." Historically, in every commoditization, the biggest winners weren't those who executed faster. They were those who redefined the game. Gutenberg wasn't the big winner—publishers and authors were. Electric companies weren't the biggest winners—Ford was. AWS is certainly a winner, but so are Airbnb and Stripe—they used commoditized infrastructure to create business models that couldn't have existed before. When coding is commoditized, winners may not be "people who code fastest with AI," but those who use zero-marginal-cost code production to redefine product forms, distribution methods, or value capture models.
After Unbundling, Re-bundling opportunities are brewing. We're in the unbundling phase now—standardized tools fragmenting, long-tail personalized tools emerging. But if historical patterns hold, these fragmented long-tail tools will eventually need a new integration layer. Maybe it's an "app store" where AI-generated one-off tools can be discovered and reused. Maybe it's a "composable platform" where users assemble long-tail tools like Lego. Maybe it's an "AI-native operating system" with code generation, execution, and management as primitive capabilities.
And electricity's 40-year lesson reminds us to be patient. Our current use of AI—making traditional software faster—is probably just the "swap steam engine for electric motor" phase. The real "assembly line moment"—redesigning the entire software paradigm around AI's characteristics—may take years or longer to arrive. But when it does, it could enable entirely new product forms that couldn't exist before:
  • One-off software: custom tools built for one specific scenario, one specific user, discarded after use.
  • Self-adaptive software: applications that generate and modify their own code in real-time based on user behavior.
  • Hyper-long-tail software: specialized products for incredibly narrow needs.
But note this: AI's development speed far exceeds the electricity era. Electricity's 40-year lag partly stemmed from physical infrastructure build-out—grids, factories, training workers all took time. AI's "infrastructure" is software and compute, iterating on monthly cycles. McKinsey's 2025 survey found organizations that redesigned end-to-end workflows before adopting AI were nearly three times more likely to see significant financial returns. This suggests: the "assembly line moment" won't wait 40 years. It might be just a few years away.

The Worst Time to Start a Company

If the analysis above is right, this is both the best and most brutal time to be a founder.
The upside is obvious: barriers to building have never been lower. One person, one weekend, a few hundred dollars in API costs can produce what used to take a team months. The distance from idea to prototype has compressed to its limit. "Can it be built" is no longer the question.
But that's exactly where hell mode begins: when everyone can build quickly, "building it" is no longer a competitive advantage. What you can build in a weekend, others can too. Today's innovation becomes tomorrow's copy.
This creates several brutal realities:
Competition intensity rises exponentially. Every lane is crowded. Low barriers mean more entrants. Fast iteration means everyone ships constantly. You're not racing a few competitors. You're racing everyone on the internet who had the same idea.
Attention becomes the ultimate bottleneck. In a supply-glut world, being seen is harder than building. Product Hunt sees dozens of new products daily. X has someone demoing a new AI tool every hour. Customer acquisition costs—paid or organic—are rising rapidly, while product differentiation is falling.
Winner-take-all effects are extreme. History shows that after every supply explosion, value concentrates intensely at the top. This means: mediocre success may disappear. Either become the category leader, or struggle in the long tail.
Moats are collapsing. Traditional software company moats—technical complexity, engineering team size, years of accumulated code—become fragile against AI. Nicolas Bustamante analyzed the top 10 moats in vertical software and their fate in the LLM era: five are collapsing (learned interfaces, custom business logic, public data access, scarce talent, bundle sales), five remain strong (proprietary data, regulatory compliance, network effects, transaction embedding, system of record status). The key insight: what's being destroyed are precisely the moats that used to block competitors from entering.

Surviving Hell Mode

So in this hell mode, what strategies might actually work?
Don't use AI to do old things. Using AI to build traditional SaaS faster is essentially swapping a steam engine for an electric motor. The question you need to ask: if code production costs zero, what product forms become possible that weren't before? One-off software? Self-adaptive software? Hyper-personalized experiences?
(Of course, short-term, "doing old things faster with AI" does offer an arbitrage window—before competitors catch on, you can capture market share with lower cost and faster speed. But this window closes quickly because what you can do, others can too.)
Build moats outside of code. If code itself is no longer a barrier, barriers can only come from: unique data assets, strong user relationships, hard-to-replicate distribution channels, or brand and community.
Speed still matters, but direction matters more than speed. In a world where everyone can execute fast, judgment—knowing what to do and for whom—becomes the real differentiator. Slowing down to think about the right problem may be more valuable than quickly executing the wrong answer.
Embrace unbundling while looking for re-bundling opportunities. This is the unbundling phase—long-tail tools emerging, standardized products fragmenting. But history tells us re-bundling is coming. Ask yourself: what integration layer will these fragmented tools eventually need? Who provides it?
Accept that this is a long game. Installation-phase chaos may last years more. This isn't a "find PMF fast then scale" era. It's an era requiring constant adaptation, constant self-reinvention. Patience and resilience may matter more than any single skill.

Out of Destruction, Creation

Every major commoditization in history came with a special kind of pain: those who accumulated advantages in the old order found those advantages evaporating. Scribes who spent a decade perfecting their handwriting became worthless in front of printing presses. Factory owners who invested fortunes in line shaft systems found them becoming liabilities in the electric age. Programmers who spent years accumulating coding skills are being caught up to by AI in months.
But the flip side of "destruction before creation" is: the disappearance of old advantages also means the disappearance of old barriers. Those previously shut out for lacking resources, lacking teams, lacking engineering capability can now compete. Things that used to require hundreds of people and tens of millions of dollars can now be started by one person in one weekend.
This is why I say we're at the dawn of change.
Not because AI will replace everyone's jobs—history shows technology rarely "eliminates" professions directly; it redefines what those professions mean. Not because everything will get better—transitions always come with pain, mismatch, and uncertainty.
But because: when a core capability gets commoditized, the entire value chain reorganizes. And the moment of value chain reorganization is precisely when new players enter and new rules get written.
Gutenberg didn't know printing would spark the Reformation. Ford didn't know the assembly line would reshape the middle class. When AWS launched in 2006, no one could foresee that companies like Airbnb and Stripe would become possible because of it.
Similarly, we don't know today what new product forms, new business models, new ways of creating value will emerge once coding is fully commoditized.
But one thing is certain: those who first understand the new rules, who first redesign themselves around new capabilities—whether individuals, teams, or companies—will have the advantage in the new order.
Out of destruction, creation.
