THE FUNDAMENTAL MISUNDERSTANDING

Here's what they don't teach you in school:
  • Probability is not about what will happen. It's about what should happen across infinite repetitions of an event.
Think about that. Read it again.
This single concept separates people who succeed from people who fail in environments with uncertainty.
And guess what? Almost everything meaningful in life involves uncertainty. Your career choices. Investment decisions. Business ventures. Relationships. Even crossing the street.

THE KELLY CRITERION: HOW MUCH TO BET

You found a +EV opportunity. Great.
Now the question: how much should you risk?
Bet too little: You're leaving money on the table. Bet too much: You'll go broke even with positive EV.
  • Enter the Kelly Criterion:
Where:
  • f∗ = fraction of bankroll to bet
  • b = net odds received
  • p = probability of winning
  • q=1−p = probability of losing
Example:
You find a bet at 3:1 odds (b=3 ), and you estimate your true win probability is 40% (p=0.40 ):
mathematica
b = 3, p = 0.40, q = 0.60

f* = (3 × 0.40 - 0.60) / 3
f* = (1.20 - 0.60) / 3
f* = 0.60 / 3
f* = 0.20  →  bet 20% of bankroll
You should bet 20% of your bankroll

The Harsh Reality of Full Kelly

Full Kelly is aggressive. Psychologically brutal. That's why professionals use fractional Kelly:
  • Half Kelly (f∗/2): More conservative, smoother growth
  • Quarter Kelly (f∗/4): Even safer, used by most pros
Why? Because your probability estimates are never perfect. You're human. You make mistakes. Fractional Kelly protects you from your own overconfidence.

BAYESIAN UPDATING: THE SUPERPOWER

The world changes. Information arrives. Winners update their beliefs. Losers don't.
Bayes' Theorem:
Translation: Your new belief should equal the likelihood of the evidence times your prior belief, divided by the probability of seeing that evidence.

Real Example: Hiring Decision

You're interviewing a candidate.
  • Prior belief: 30% of applicants with this resume are actually good → P(A)=0.30
  • New evidence: They crushed the technical interview
  • Likelihood: 80% of good candidates crush the interview → P(B|A)=0.80
  • Base rate: 35% of all candidates crush the interview → P(B)=0.35
New belief:
P(Good|Crushed) = ( 0.80 × 0.30 ) / 0.35 ≈ 0.686 → 68.6%
You went from 30% confidence to 69% confidence with one piece of evidence. That's Bayesian updating.

THE VARIANCE PROBLEM

Here's the truth nobody wants to hear:
Short-term results are almost entirely luck. Long-term results are almost entirely skill
Even the best player in the world can have losing months. Years, even. Because:
ObservedOutcome=Skill+VarianceObserved Outcome=Skill+Variance
Over 100 hands: Variance dominates Over 100,000 hands: Skill dominates
This applies to everything:
  • Your startup might fail due to bad timing (variance)
  • Your investment might lose money this year (variance)
  • Your correct decision might have a bad outcome (variance)
The key: Make +EV decisions and accept the variance.

THE COMPOUNDING PROBLEM: GAMBLER'S RUIN

Here's where most people fail. They understand EV. They make +EV decisions. But they still go broke.
Probability of ruin formula:
P(ruin) = ((1-p) / p) ^ (Bankroll / Bet size)
For p=0.55, betting 25% of bankroll each time:
P(ruin) = (0.45 / 0.55) ^ 4 ≈ 0.45 → 45%
45% chance of going broke even with a 55% win rate!
Now try Half Kelly (6% of bankroll):
P(ruin) ≈ 0.02 → 2%
Only 2% chance of ruin

THE POWER OF SAMPLE SIZE

Most people make decisions based on tiny samples. This is statistical suicide.
SE=σ/√nSE = σ / √n
To halve your error, you need 4x the data. To reduce error by 10x, you need 100x the data.
Practical Example: You test a campaign. After 20 conversions, you see 15% conversion rate. Your 95% confidence interval:
CI=p±1.96×√(p(1−p)/n)CI = p ± 1.96 × √(p(1-p)/n)
CI=0.15±1.96×√(0.15×0.85/20)=[−0.01,0.31]CI = 0.15 ± 1.96 × √(0.15 × 0.85 / 20) = [-0.01, 0.31]
The true rate could be 1% or 31%. You know almost nothing.
With 500 conversions at 15%, your CI narrows to [11.8%, 18.2%]. Now you actually know something.

THE POWER LAW DISTRIBUTION

Most outcomes in life don't follow normal distributions. They follow power laws: P(x) = C × x^(-α)
  • Wealth distribution: α ≈ 1.5
  • City sizes: α ≈ 2.0
  • Startup outcomes: α ≈ 1.2
In power law distributions, outliers dominate everything. The top 1% own 50%+ of the total. The median is far below the mean.
Implication for life: In winner-take-all environments, taking many shots with asymmetric upside is the optimal strategy. One 1000x winner offsets 999 losses.

THE COMPOUNDING EDGE

Small edges compound exponentially:
Final=Initial×(1+r)nFinal = Initial × (1 + r)^n
A 2% edge per decision, across 100 decisions:
(1.02)^100 ≈ 7.24x
A 2% edge turns into 624% returns. But one catastrophic error cuts that in half:
(1.02)^95 × 0.5 ≈ 3.37x
Key principle: Avoid ruin first. Optimize for edge second.

THE PARADOX OF SKILL

As competition increases and everyone gets better, luck becomes more important in determining outcomes.
Observedperformance=Skill+LuckObserved performance=Skill+Luck
As skill variance decreases (everyone studies the same GTO charts, reads the same books), luck variance stays constant. So luck's share of outcomes grows.
This is why the best don't always win. And why you need to focus on process, not outcomes.
