Reinforcement Learning on Polymarket: What Happens When the Bot Writes Its Own Rules
156K
My Bot Lost Money for 14 Days. Then It Figured Out Something I Couldn't. Most traders on Polymarket use supervised learning: train a model on historical data, predict outcomes, hope the pattern holds. LSTM, XGBoost, Random Forest. It works until it doesn't. I wanted something different. Instead of teaching a bot what to predict, I let it figure out how to trade on its own. I gave it $2,000 in starting capital, connected it to live Polymarket data, and set one rule: maximize cumulative P&L. No labels, no correct answers. Just reward for profit and penalty for loss. After 6 months of live trading, the system has been consistently profitable. This article breaks down the full architecture: the math, the training process, the mistakes, and what the agent learned that I never explicitly programmed. Disclaimer: Not Financial Advice & Do Your Own Research & Markets involve risk.

Part I: Why Supervised Learning Fails

Supervised learning optimizes for prediction accuracy. But in trading, accuracy and profit are not the same thing. A model can be right 68% of the time and still lose money if it ignores execution costs.
The fundamental difference between the two approaches:
(1) Supervised learning minimizes error. RL maximizes cumulative discounted reward.
Consider this: your model predicts YES at 68% confidence. Market price is 0.65. Edge = 0.03. But the effective spread is 0.04. Every trade costs more than the edge it captures. Supervised learning has no mechanism to account for this.
RL solves this naturally. Because the agent receives real P&L as its reward signal, it learns to account for spreads, slippage, and position sizing automatically. It discovers when to wait, how much to risk, and when to exit early. These behaviors emerge without explicit programming.

Part II: Markov Decision Process

Every RL problem starts with defining the environment as a Markov Decision Process: what the agent sees (state), what it can do (actions), and what it gets back (reward). Getting these right determines everything. State Space S(t) The state is everything the agent observes at decision time. I used 47 features organized into four groups:
2) State vector: 18 market + 12 model + 10 portfolio + 7 temporal features.
Market features capture the current order book state: prices, volumes, spread, microprice, volatility. Model features include an XGBoost fair-value estimate and news sentiment scores. Portfolio features track the agent's own position and risk. Temporal features encode time-of-day and time-to-resolution. Action Space A
I used 7 discrete actions with fixed position sizes. Discrete actions are simpler to optimize and converged 3x faster than continuous alternatives in my testing:
(3) Seven discrete actions. Size granularity (5%/15%/30%) calibrated over 10,000 episodes.
Reward Function R(s, a, s')
The reward function is where most RL trading systems succeed or fail. After 14 iterations of redesign, I landed on a six-component signal that teaches the agent both what to do and what to avoid:
(4) Six-component reward. Drawdown penalty (0.5) is the strongest negative signal.
The wait reward (+0.01 for HOLD when no edge exists) was the single most impactful addition. Without it, the agent overtrades at 40+ times per day. With it, trades drop to 8-12 per day on high-confidence setups only.
(5) Average dollar contribution of each reward component per trade.

Part III: PPO Algorithm

I chose Proximal Policy Optimization because it offers the best stability-to-performance ratio for this problem. DQN collapsed after 8K episodes, A2C showed unstable oscillations. PPO has been stable from episode 1. Clipped Surrogate Objective The core innovation of PPO is preventing catastrophic policy updates. It clips the probability ratio so that no single gradient step can change the policy by more than 20%:
(6) Probability ratio clipped to [0.8, 1.2]. Guarantees stable training.
Generalized Advantage Estimation GAE provides a smooth estimate of how much better (or worse) a specific action was compared to the average. The high gamma (0.995) is critical here because Polymarket rewards are often delayed by days:
(7) GAE with high discount factor. Rewards delayed by days require patient credit assignment.
Total Loss The agent optimizes three objectives simultaneously: the policy (clip loss), the value function (prediction accuracy), and an entropy bonus that prevents the agent from converging too early to a single strategy:
(8) Combined loss: policy optimization + value learning + exploration.
Network Architecture Actor-Critic architecture with a shared feature extractor. The shared layers learn market representations, then split into a policy head (which action to take) and a value head (how good is this state):
(9) Shared backbone with separate actor and critic heads. LayerNorm for sequential data.

Part IV: Environment & Execution

The training environment simulates Polymarket with realistic friction: order book dynamics, spread costs, slippage proportional to trade size, and market impact. Without these, the agent learns an idealized version of trading that fails immediately in production. Execution Model
(11) Execution price: midprice + half-spread + volume-dependent slippage.
Data
Training data covers 14,247 resolved Polymarket markets from January 2023 to December 2025. I collected full order book snapshots every 30 seconds via Polygon RPC, totaling 847 GB of raw data compressed to 92 GB of features.
The train/test split is temporal: everything before October 2025 for training (12,100 markets), October through December 2025 for validation (2,147 markets). The agent never saw any validation data during training.

Part V: What The Agent Learned

After 50,000 training episodes, the agent developed four distinct behaviors that I did not explicitly program. Each emerged purely from the reward signal.

1. Patience

The most dramatic change was learning when not to trade. In the first 5,000 episodes, the agent traded 30-40 times per day. By episode 30,000, it traded 8-12 times. The HOLD action grew from 30% to 62% of all decisions.
(12) Action distribution over 26 weeks. HOLD (green line) becomes dominant.

2. Timing Sensitivity

The agent discovered on its own that mispricings concentrate in the hours before market resolution. It now executes 78% of trades in the last 6 hours and almost none in the first 24 hours of a new market:
(13) Trade probability conditional on time to close. Agent learned timing from reward alone.

3. Learned Kelly Criterion

Without any knowledge of the Kelly formula, the agent independently converged to a position-sizing strategy that closely mirrors it. High-confidence setups get large positions, uncertain setups get minimal exposure:
(14) Theoretical Kelly vs agent observed behavior. Convergence without explicit programming.

4. Category Specialization

By episode 20,000, the agent reduced crypto market trading to just 3% of activity. It concentrated on weather (34%) and geopolitics (28%) where edges persist longer and competition is lower. The agent discovered market efficiency differences without being told about them.

Weekly Self-Retraining

To keep the policy adapted to changing market conditions, the agent retrains every Sunday at 3 AM on the previous 4 weeks of live data. If the new policy shows worse Sharpe on held-out data, the update is automatically rejected:

Part VII: Infrastructure

The full system runs on a Mac Mini M2 with 16GB RAM. Python 3.11, PyTorch 2.1, and Stable-Baselines3 for the RL framework. Custom Gymnasium environment for simulation. py-clob-client for Polymarket CLOB API connection. Redis for state caching and ClickHouse for 847 GB of order book history. Telegram bot for real-time alerts. Total monthly cost: $47.

Kill Switches

Risk management runs independently of the agent. Daily drawdown exceeding 5% pauses trading until the next day. Hourly drawdown above 3% triggers a 2-hour pause. Three consecutive losses above $500 require manual review. Inference latency above 100ms causes the trade to be skipped. The system has been paused 7 times in 180 days. All resolved automatically except two.
